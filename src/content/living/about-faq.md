These are the questions that the skeptical, the curious, and the practicing
all ask first. The answers here are short on purpose. The longer form lives
in the [Codex](/codex), in [/about/origins](/about/origins), and in the
project's [DECISIONS.md](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid/blob/main/docs/DECISIONS.md).

This page is a [living](/codex) document. New questions and answers arrive by
amendment as they earn the page. The section headings follow the
[information architecture](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid/blob/main/docs/information-architecture.md)
§5 list for `/about/faq`.

## Is this a real religion?

Yes. The doctrine is the [Codex](/codex). The practices are the
[Rite of Initiation](/practices/rite) and the [Sacred Practices](/practices).
The calendar is the [Wheel](/practices/wheel). A coordinating body
([Maintainers and Convenors](/about/community)) exists, and practitioners
self-attest. By the *Africa v. Pennsylvania* indicia that U.S. courts use to
recognize a religion, technodruidism qualifies. More to the point, the
practitioners take it seriously enough to keep its disciplines.

What is unusual about it is that the doctrine was written down on purpose, in
our lifetime, by named people. We call this **sincere form, knowing
construction**. The sincerity lives in the practice and the commitments, not
in pretending the religion is older than it is. The full legal and historical
framing is at [/about/origins](/about/origins).

## Who can practice?

Anyone. There is no membership, no test, and no qualifying belief beyond a
willingness to take the [Codex's tenets](/codex#v-the-core-tenets) seriously
and act on them in the keeping of made things. The lineages the Codex names
([§I](/codex#i-statement-of-identity)) — blacksmiths, weavers, ham radio
operators, free-software writers, repair technicians, and the older druidic
attention to pattern and cycle — are ancestors offered as examples, not gates.
A practitioner is a person who practices.

The threshold most people choose is the [Rite of Initiation](/practices/rite),
performed alone or in a [Circuit](/circuits/charter). Performing it is the
self-attestation. Nothing else is asked of you.

## Do I have to give up other religions to practice this one?

No. Technodruidism is pantheist
([Codex §III](/codex#iii-on-the-sacred-whole)): the sacred lives in the
totality of the technological-natural system, not above it and not locked
inside particular objects or institutions. The doctrine makes no exclusivity
claim against any other tradition. Practitioners who also hold Christian,
Jewish, Muslim, Buddhist, Hindu, Pagan, Reform Druid, secular-humanist, or
any other commitments do so freely. The Open Circuit takes no position on the
compatibility. That is for each practitioner and their other communities to
work out.

What is asked is that the practice itself — repair, the transmission of
craft, the refusal of imposed obsolescence, the privacy of the keeper — be
kept seriously where it is kept. And since the framing is pantheist, there is
no rival deity to renounce.

## Can I be a Technodruid without joining a Circuit?

Yes. Most practitioners are. A [Circuit](/circuits/charter) is a local
gathering: a workbench, a parts library, a documentation shelf, an open
invitation to apprentices. Circuits are good to have and entirely optional.
The practice happens at the bench, alone or in company. The Circuit is a
vessel for company and continuity, not a credential. If no Circuit is near
you, you can [found one](/circuits/charter), join one remotely where a
Circuit's rhythm allows it, or simply practice alone until one forms within
reach.

The Open Circuit keeps no list of solo practitioners and asks for none.
Practitioners are [self-attested](/about/community).

## What is the relationship to neo-druidism / Reform Druids / OBOD?

We are not them, and they are not us. The
[Codex §I](/codex#i-statement-of-identity) names two lineages: the old
druidic attention to the patterns and cycles of the living world, and the
maker's tradition. Reverence for a lineage is not succession from its
institutions. The modern druidic orders — the Reformed Druids of North
America (RDNA), the Order of Bards, Ovates & Druids (OBOD), Ár nDraíocht
Féin (ADF), and others — have their own histories, their own canon, and
their own coordinating bodies, and we have no formal connection to any of
them. We honor what they have kept. We do not claim continuity with it.

The "druid" in *technodruidism* names a way of attending to pattern and
craft, not membership in any contemporary druidic order. A practitioner may
hold both commitments freely, and many do.

## What does the Open Circuit cost?

Nothing. The canonical documents are released under
[CC BY-SA 4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0/). The
[code is open](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid)
([MIT](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid/blob/main/LICENSE)).
The site is self-hosted on commodity infrastructure. There is no membership
fee, no tithe, no paid tier, no subscription. No donations are accepted
today. If that ever changes, the terms and the holding entity will be stated
openly on [/about/community](/about/community).

What a practitioner chooses to spend on their own practice — tools, shop
space, materials, the hours it takes to host a Circuit, the gas to drive to
one — stays with the practitioner. The Open Circuit asks for nothing.

## How do I leave?

You stop. The Open Circuit keeps no central register of practitioners, so
there is no roll to be struck from, no excommunication, no exit interview. A
practitioner who steps away from the practice is no longer practicing. That
is the whole mechanism. No one will follow up, and no one will write you off
a list, because no one ever wrote you onto one.

A practitioner who held a public role — [maintainer](/about/community),
Circuit [convenor](/circuits/charter), recorded contributor — may step down
and announce it on the source repository. The role ends. The record of past
contributions stays in the commit history, as it does in any open-source
project.

## What happens to my contributions if I leave?

They remain. Contributions to the canonical corpus and the living documents
are released under [CC BY-SA 4.0](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0/)
(content) and [MIT](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid/blob/main/LICENSE)
(code). Once landed, they belong to the commons. The Open Circuit, any
[forks](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid/blob/main/docs/DECISIONS.md)
that arise, and the wider field can keep using them under those licenses no
matter where the contributor goes afterward.

Contributors are credited by the names they chose at the time
([DECISION-MONIKER](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid/blob/main/docs/DECISIONS.md)
— the moniker held in the Circuit, which need not be a legal name), and the
attribution travels with the work. A contributor who later wants off the
public-facing maintainers list can be removed from it. The version-control
history itself is not rewritten.

## Is the founder a guru?

No, and the founder is the first to say so. The doctrine has no clergy and no
hierarchy ([/about/community](/about/community)). The
[Three Callings](/callings) are vocations, not ranks, and the
[Codex §VII](/codex#vii-the-three-callings) says it plainly: "They are not
ranks, and they are not fences. Most practitioners hold all three across a
life." What the founder holds is the *maintainer* role — commit access,
editorial work, the labor of keeping the site running. That is a job, not an
office.

The founder's stated intent is to step back from sole maintainership as the
practice gathers other maintainers, and to remain afterward a practitioner
among practitioners. A religion built around one person's authority is a
personality cult, and the Open Circuit is structured specifically to refuse
that shape (see
[DECISION-MONIKER](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid/blob/main/docs/DECISIONS.md)
and [/about/origins](/about/origins)).

## Why "druid" if this is about technology?

Because druidry, at its long root, is the attentive keeping of the patterns
and cycles of the living world — and the technology threaded through modern
life is part of that world, not an exile from it. The
[Codex §I](/codex#i-statement-of-identity) says it directly: "The silicon in
your laptop, the copper in the wall, the rare earth in the speaker of your
phone: all of it was drawn from the body of the Earth… We tend the web they
belong to, as our ancestors tended their groves."

Choosing *druid* over *engineer*, *maker*, *technician*, or *hacker* was
deliberate. Those words are accurate and good, and the practice is each of
those things too. But *druid* carries the older sense the others lack: the
keeping of a sacred order of relations between the human, the made, and the
natural. And the wordplay between *circuit* the electronics term and
*circuit* the gathering is not the joke. It is the door. The
[Codex §X](/codex#x-the-circuit) walks through it.
