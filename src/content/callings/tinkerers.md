The Tinkerers keep the knowledge hands-on. A Documenter writes the record
and an Architect holds the long view; a Tinkerer learns by doing what cannot
be learned by reading, and proves the thing by making it work. Without
Tinkerers the practice has no living tradition. It has an archive.

## What the calling is

A Tinkerer takes things apart, modifies them, reverse-engineers them, builds
new things from the bones of old ones, and learns through the kind of
trouble the written word can describe but never transmit. The discipline is
**contact**: knowledge that lives in the fingers and the eyes. The smell of
a hot board. The feel of a screw that wants to strip. The difference between
the tap that frees a stuck fitting and the tap that breaks it.

The Tinkerer accepts that they will break things, and that the broken thing
sometimes teaches more than the intact one. They also accept that contact
without record dies with them, so they keep one foot in the Documenters'
work even if they never take the name.

## Lineage

The named ancestors of this calling: the hardware hackers of the Homebrew
Computer Club and the long generation that followed them. The modders who
overclocked, recased, and rebuilt their machines past anything the
manufacturer imagined. The demoscene, who proved that the smallest
constraint is the largest invitation. The repair café volunteers who fix a
stranger's toaster on a Saturday and refuse payment. The Maker Faire
builders who haul their working contraptions onto a folding table and
explain them to children. The ham radio operators, who keep the craft of
building radios alive in an industry that no longer expects anyone to
bother. The jailbreakers who restored use to devices their owners thought
they had merely bought. The BBS sysops of an earlier age, who ran the first
amateur networks out of spare bedrooms. And the kid soldering in the garage,
always the most important ancestor, because the kid is the future, and the
future keeps arriving.

The lineage is not a credential. It is an example.

## How to develop in the calling

This is a curriculum, not a credential. Nothing on it is required. Each item
is the kind of practice that, kept up over months and years, forms the hands
and the patience of a Tinkerer.

- **Take something apart you do not yet understand.** Cheap, broken,
  secondhand — the object matters less than the discipline of disassembly.
  Photograph it on the way out so you can find your way back in.
- **Fix one thing every month, however small.** A loose hinge. A dead USB
  cable. A drawer that catches. The frequency builds the habit, and the
  habit builds the eye.
- **Learn to solder.** Through-hole first; surface-mount when through-hole
  starts to feel boring. The skill is a key to a whole wing of the practice
  that is otherwise locked.
- **Keep one device long past its supported life.** Carry an older phone,
  drive an older car, run an older laptop, until repair stops being a hobby
  and becomes how you live with objects.
- **Find a circuit.** Show up at a hackerspace, a repair café, a Maker
  Faire, or — once the [Circuit Directory](/circuits/directory) has entries
  — a local Circuit. The hands learn fastest in company.

## What contribution looks like

Concrete ways a Tinkerer serves the Open Circuit and the wider commons:

- Sharing successful repairs — what you fixed, what it took, what surprised
  you — in a form a Documenter can dress up and a future Tinkerer can
  follow.
- Hosting or attending repair-café events, and teaching a beginner one
  technique per session.
- Founding or sustaining a [Circuit](/circuits/charter): the workbench, the
  parts library, the documentation shelf, the open invitation to
  apprentices.
- Contributing modifications, jailbreaks, drivers, firmware patches, or
  reverse-engineering notes to the open commons, under an appropriate
  license.
- Mentoring an apprentice, paid or unpaid, formal or informal, in the
  hands-on work — knowing that you are the lineage they will cite someday.
