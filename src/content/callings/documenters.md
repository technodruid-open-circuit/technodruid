The Documenters keep the knowledge findable. A Tinkerer's pride is the
working device on the bench. An Architect's pride is the long view that
bends to no fashion. A Documenter's pride is the record that outlives both:
the page a stranger lands on five years from now, when their own version of
the same problem arrives.

This page is a [living](/codex) document. Its section headings follow the
[information architecture](https://github.com/technodruid-open-circuit/technodruid/blob/main/docs/information-architecture.md)
§5 list for `/callings/[name]`.

## What the calling is

A Documenter writes the repair guides, records the techniques, tends the
wikis, shoots the video tutorials, updates the README, and posts the
teardown. The work is unglamorous, and it compounds: every clean record
makes the next fix faster for someone who was never in the room. The
discipline is **capture**. What lives in one head dies with that head. What
is written down outlives the writer, and outlives the device.

The Documenter accepts that some of their work will be wrong, will go stale,
and will be corrected by strangers. They accept it because the alternative,
leaving the knowledge unwritten, is worse than being wrong in public.

## Lineage

The named ancestors of this calling: the iFixit contributors who built the
largest open repair-manual archive in the world. The technical writers who
made manufacturer documentation legible to people not on the company
payroll. The archivists of dead platforms — the abandoned-OS librarians, the
floppy-image curators, the people saving forum threads before the host
shutters. The hackerspace wiki gardeners who keep the inventory page and
the laser-cutter SOP and the membership rules current. And every patient
soul who has ever explained their work in public.

The lineage is not a credential. It is an example.

## How to develop in the calling

This is a curriculum, not a credential. Nothing on it is required. Each item
is the kind of practice that, kept up over months and years, forms the eye
and the hand of a Documenter.

- **Write down the next thing you fix.** Title, date, device, steps, what
  went wrong, what you would do differently. Photograph each step before you
  disturb it. Publish the result somewhere public, even if no one reads it.
  The practice is the curriculum.
- **Adopt a wiki.** Find a documentation surface that needs a gardener — an
  iFixit guide that is wrong, a hackerspace inventory page gone stale, a
  README untouched since the project began — and tend it for a season.
- **Read the field's standards.** RFC 1925, *The Pragmatic Programmer*, the
  Diátaxis documentation framework, the iFixit Technical Writing Project
  style guide. Read them less for the rules than for the shape of the
  attention behind them.
- **Practice the inverted pyramid.** The reader who needs step three is
  rarely the reader who needs the introduction. Write so any single
  paragraph can stand on its own.
- **Adopt one device, one technique, or one tradition** and become its
  public record-keeper. For the first decade, depth beats breadth.

## What contribution looks like

Concrete ways a Documenter serves the Open Circuit and the wider commons:

- Writing repair walkthroughs for `/library/repair`: original work, clearly
  attributed, with the practitioner's name, the device, the date, and the
  outcome.
- Tending sections of `/library/reading`: annotating existing entries,
  proposing new ones with the reasoning spelled out, marking what has fallen
  out of print.
- Editing the canonical documents through public proposals. Documenters are
  the ones who catch the typo, the broken cross-reference, the section that
  has drifted out of step with its sibling.
- Translating canonical or living documents. Translation is documentation in
  another tongue.
- Contributing to iFixit, to Repair Café International's event
  documentation, to the Internet Archive's software collections, to
  hackerspace wikis, or to any of the long-running archival projects that
  hold the commons.
