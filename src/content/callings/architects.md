The Architects keep the knowledge in proportion. A Documenter writes the
record and a Tinkerer keeps the practice alive in the hands; an Architect
holds the doctrine, minds the commons, weighs the politics of repair and the
ethics of attention, and thinks across timescales the busy moment cannot
see. Without Architects the practice loses its through-line, the thread that
ties today's fix to the long question of what gets to be repaired at all.

## What the calling is

An Architect holds the doctrine, minds the commons, weighs the politics of
repair and the ethics of attention, and takes the long view on behalf of
people not yet present. The discipline is **distance**. Distance is not
aloofness. The Architect's work is to ask the questions the workshop is too
busy to ask, and to keep asking them in public.

The Architect accepts the calling's own dangers: that distance can curdle
into detachment, and that distance without contact produces theory nobody
needs. They also accept that the calling carries no rank. An Architect
without Tinkerers is a thinker without a workshop, and an Architect who has
never written a usable record is a voice the commons owes no attention.

## Lineage

The named ancestors of this calling: the free-software philosophers —
Richard Stallman, Lawrence Lessig, Eben Moglen, and the writers after them
who refined and dissented. The hackerspace founders who built c-base,
Metalab, NYC Resistor, and Noisebridge, and taught a generation what a third
place for makers could be. The right-to-repair advocates: Aaron
Perzanowski, the iFixit policy team, the Repair Association, the
legislative coalitions in the EU and the US. The conservators of historic
computing at the Computer History Museum, the Centre for Computing History,
and the Internet Archive's software collections. And the long teachers —
Ursula Franklin, Ivan Illich, Lewis Hyde, and many less named — who shaped
maker culture by example more than by office.

The lineage is not a credential. It is an example.

## How to develop in the calling

This is a curriculum, not a credential. Nothing on it is required. Each item
is the kind of practice that, kept up over years and decades, forms the
patience and the proportion of an Architect.

- **Read the field's long thinkers.** Begin with the entries in
  [/library/reading](/library/reading) and follow the citations outward. The
  calling is the inheritance of a reading practice as much as a writing
  practice.
- **Write the question down before answering it.** Architecture is mostly
  the discipline of asking the right question, in a form a community can act
  on. Most public writing skips this step.
- **Take a long view in public.** At intervals, write on a question whose
  answer will not change for a decade: the politics of a platform, the
  trajectory of a craft, what one generation of makers owes the next.
- **Sit with disagreement.** The calling is not about being right. It is
  about being articulate enough that disagreement can sharpen the question.
  Cultivate the kind of writing that invites response rather than
  concession.
- **Hold a doctrinal seat sparingly.** Maintainership of canonical
  documents, a seat on a hackerspace board, the mentorship of an apprentice
  over years — these are Architect roles by service, not by title. Decline
  them when you are not the right keeper. Accept them when you are.

## What contribution looks like

Concrete ways an Architect serves the Open Circuit and the wider commons:

- Proposing amendments to the canonical documents — the Codex, the Creed,
  the Rite of Initiation, the Founding Charter — through the public-proposal
  process described at [/about/community](/about/community).
- Maintaining or moderating the project's discussions and review processes,
  holding moderation as a service rather than a position.
- Writing essays, treatises, or position pieces for the broader commons: the
  next *Shop Class as Soulcraft*, *The Real World of Technology*, or *Free
  Software, Free Society*, in the writer's own voice and moment.
- Sustaining advocacy alongside the right-to-repair coalitions and
  free-software foundations listed in [/library/links](/library/links).
- Teaching in the long form — residencies, conference talks, classrooms,
  apprenticeships held over years — with the aim of shaping the next
  generation of all three callings, not just their own.
